Consumption, resources, and the environment: What you need to know

The Problem
    The world population currently stands at 7.7 billion, and is projected to reach almost ten billion halfway through the century (un.org). With population on the rise and standards of living on the rise, the resource strain on planet Earth grows day by day. Consumption on a global scale is not evenly distributed; Worldwatch says “The 12 percent of the world’s population that lives in North America and Western Europe accounts for 60 percent of private consumption spending, while the one-third living in South Asia and sub-Saharan Africa accounts for only 3.2 percent” (worldwatch.org). While developed nations continue to trend upwards in consumption, many citizens of developing nations look to developed standards of living and strive to match those by having family cars, smartphones, and other veblen goods.
    “Supply and demand” is perhaps the most commonly known economic saying. The demands created by the pursuit of luxury force a stream of supply that is not only unsustainable in the finite limits of the resources being extracted, but also in the damages its causing the Earth. The issue of where resource extraction, refining, and manufacturing of products take place is a big issue; no one wants the water and soil damages of copper mining, nor the smog of burning coal and manufacturing plants, to be in their own backyard. As a result, the dirty work is often outsourced to developing countries where environmental issues doe not take priority, and the business is greatly needed. Fossil fuel usage in meeting everyday demand contributes eighty-seven percent of all human caused carbon emissions. Others include land use changes, which contribute nine percent of human emissions, and industrial processes, which cause four percent (whatsyourimpact.org).

What We Can Do
    The slogan “reduce, reuse, recycle,” which itself has been reused many times, addresses the heart of the issue. Reducing means using consuming less, limiting consumption and avoiding frivolous purchases, and shifting your paradigm to be content with less. However, reducing is not only directed at the consumer stage. Reducing at the product creation stage can yield great impacts as well. Industries can reduce their impacts by improving material use efficiency, extending the life of their products (and letting go of planned obsolescence), and material substitution. Consumers and industry can also use an LCA (life cycle assessment) to determine the most efficient and least environmentally impactful products to use or recommend to others. An LCA considers the materials put into the product, the energy used in manufacturing, its utility, and its environmental impact after its life is over and it is disposed of.
    Reusing and recycling are important because they both entail keeping the refined materials in the loop of production and consumption thereby reducing the amount of resources we need to extract from nature. Recycling of metals like copper and iron have made an impact, albeit small, on how much we need to extract from the Earth. The downside of recycling metals, and other things, is the contamination and degradation of the metal being recycled. Inevitable impurities are added at each recycling and detracts from the overall quality of the metal. None of the approaches currently being taken by themselves can overcome the resource depletion and environmental pollution our consumption causes today, but a combination of all of these, with a critical step of lowering consumption, can lessen the burden on planet Earth.

How We Approach a Solution: Responsible Farming Through Certification
    The need for lumber is not going away, neither are the problems that come with plantation farming of trees. The solution then lies in approaching plantations in a more responsible manner. This is already pursued by forest owners when they seek certification for their forests. Certification organizations will use third party auditors to grade how forestry is being conducted based on a set of indicators. These indicators will look at GMOs trees in forests, harvesting and clear cutting practices. In return for seeking certification, certified forests are able to sell lumber to a wider variety of vendors, including those who require higher standards for sustainability and forestry management.


Money Doesn’t Grow on Trees… but Wood Does
Trees, just like any other terrestrial plant, turns seemingly insignificant ingredients, like sunlight, water, and nutrients from the soil. However, trees are vital to society because of their strong fibrous structure. Trees like northern red oak are known for their strength. As a result they are widely used in building furniture and flooring. Woods like pine are vital to the construction of house-frames. With the increase in demand for wood in everyday object came the supply of wood in larger quantities. To achieve this, man had to supercede nature’s pace by artificially creating plantations of trees. Proper silviculture is essential for growing trees efficiently and responsibly. While a natural forest can take generations before it is ready for harvest (forty to fifty years), a properly managed plantation can be ready for harvest in about twenty-five to forty years (BBE course materials). The silvicultural practice of “thinning” is one of the methods that allow for such prodigious production of trees. Thinning means to get rid of the smaller trees in the forest to make room for the bigger trees and decrease the competition. Thinning is typically done with chemicals that will kill the smaller trees.

The Efficiencies that Plantations Provide are Balanced by Drawbacks
    The entire process of plantation farming for trees causes many contradictions with natural processes; Plantations harm the natural ecosystem and strains the soil.
The plantation setup requires space to be cleared. Typically a natural forest is cleared to make space for this plantation. Then, an optimal species is picked out for planting. The tree species is chosen based on market need and the climate conditions. Trees are planted in close proximity to maximize output and efficiency. The close proximity of the trees (using 12’ x 12’ spacing in the picture) would crowd out and outcompete with smaller plants for resources (chatoongariver). Notice the image above, there is little diversity in tree sizes, herbaceous plants, or other shelter that animals can use. This creates a homogeneity that is not conducive to maintaining the natural ecosystem.
    The constant growing and harvesting of trees in one area also causes soil damages. Nutrients taken out of the soil by trees, like nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium (dpi), are not naturally replaced; in a natural forest, plants that die will decay and provide nutrients to the soil for future succession.

Wood: the Good, the Bad, and the "Neutral"

What is good about a shift to using wood for construction materials? Or growing trees? Or harvesting them? Trees and forests play a big role in the carbon cycle. With the large surface area of their foliage, they take in carbon dioxide from the atmosphere to make glucose for themselves. In doing so, they are sequestering, or storing, carbon in their limbs. This carbon can be released in one of three different ways: a plant naturally dies and as it rots, the carbon is released back into the atmosphere. The tree could be used to make a bio-product. This product will sequester the carbon because the carbon is an integral part of the material. As long as the product lasts and the wood retains integrity, the vast majority of the carbon in the wood used will be sequestered in the product. Finally, carbon can also be released into the atmosphere through combustion reactions like burning as a biofuel or in a forest fire. Ultimately, using wood contributes to carbon sequestration which could even alleviate the carbon released by dead and rotting trees in nature.
Oftentimes, wood is a much more eco-friendly building material than concrete and steel which give off much more carbon dioxide and other harmful substances in the manufacturing process (umass). As a building material, wood provides more flexibility because of the wide variety of hard and soft woods that can be used. Hardwoods like Texas Ebony can be used for building framing because they are very rigid and structurally stable. Softwoods line pine are often used in furniture and paper. The differing physical properties make it so different woods are well suited for their tasks. Although natural wood is already a solid material, through experimentation, we are even further expanding on its uses. Processed wood materials like paper (which uses clay), glulam (which uses plastic), and other cool emerging products.



Cutting a tree down causes carbon debt, and depending on how the tree is used, this debt could be quickly accrued (burning the tree for energy), or slowly taken on (when a house is demolished after a century). Offsetting this carbon debt requires planting more trees. Another reason to rely less on trees is the tedious processing involved. This can be seen from the LCA of most wood products. On the right is a diagram of the cradle to gate LCA of wood (Image source: BBE Course Material). As opposed to a cradle to grave, the cradle to gate stops at production. Even with only the cradle to gate LCA, it can be seen that this is an energy intensive process.

Big Question: BUT is using wood actually good?
Wood is a critical player in carbon neutrality, when harvested for use in energy or as a building material, it is taking away that carbon uptake ability from nature. On the other hand, if construction needs to be done, it is better that wood is used rather than steel or concrete.

Why the Shift to Bioplastics and How it’s made
    Plastic, since its creation, has offered people endless utility at the price of causing harm to the future. While many plastic products used in building materials or computer hardware have a greater ratio of use to time as waste. Other plastic like plastic packaging and plastic bags have a low ratio of use to lifespan; plastic bags are used an average of 12 minutes (biologicaldiversity.org) before they are disposed of and can take up to 1,000 years to decompose (greentumble.com). In addition to this, plastic bags left to nature has broken down and spread as microplastics, entered our food and water systems, and ingestion by animals. An alternative to traditional polyethylene plastic bags are compostable bioplastic bags which can decompose.
    Compostable bioplastic bags, by standard, are able to be 60% decomposed within 180 days (BBE). These are plastic bags made from plant materials. Feedstock, like corn and sugarcane or wood chips, that are pretreated undergo either milling (for corn and sugarcane) or enzymatic hydrolysis (wood chips) to extract sugar. The sugars extracted from plants are used as a microbial food source for the process of fermentation. Fermentation to make the bioproducts for plastic comes from genetically modified yeast that yields a large amount of the bioproduct as a byproduct when it consumes the sugar. Bioproducts like succinic acid can be used to create bioplastics (BBE).

What its impacts are
    While the lasting impacts of a compostable bioplastic bag may be shorter than a traditional polyethylene plastic bag. The bioplastic bag has other drawbacks, namely the environmental cost of manufacturing, and the environmental cost of disposal. The compostable plastic bag uses the most oil and gas fuel which makes its manufacturing process more impactful.

The Future of Bioplastics
    Many cities around the world, Minneapolis included, are considering a complete and total shift away from polyethylene plastic bags because of their environmental harm post use. In order to do this, the right facilities for manufacturing bioplastic and processing bioplastic wastes have to be put in place. Once in place, composting bioplastics could also serve as a way to return nutrients to the soil to boost agricultural outputs. They would facilitate returning compostable food waste to the soil.
In order to accommodate a wide-scale adoption of compostable plastics, facilities that can handle the anaerobic composting process would have to be put in place. This expansion of infrastructure has already started in Europe where the number of plants have increased from three in 1990 to 290 in 2015. In practice, compostable plastic bags are a great way to lessen the long term negative impacts that consumerism has on the planet. The trick to making this better than traditional plastic bags is to offset the drawbacks of higher consumption in manufacturing and controlling the harmful gases produced in the composting process.

"Bioenergy is the future! Yes or No?

      Bioenergy is the past, and it seems like we are moving towards making it the future as well. Wood was one of the first sources of bioenergy used. It was burned to provide light, warmth, and for cooking foods (forestryfocus). In successive eras, oil, coal, and natural gas have largely taken the place of wood in providing energy for developed countries (forestryfocus). However, taking sequestered carbon in coal, oil, and natural gas, and releasing it into the atmosphere as byproduct greenhouse gas emissions in the process of creating energy has caused severe climate change that will be hard to recover from (greentumble). Bioenergy claims to address this problem by only releasing the recently sequestered carbon (like in tree wood) instead of putting carbon from below ground into the atmosphere. However, even using trees has a short term negative effect on the atmosphere because it simultaneously puts carbon into the atmosphere while also taking away the very source of carbon uptake that would balance the extra carbon: trees! In addition to this dilemma, there is also the fact that cutting down forests for energy is impractical because wood has a lower EROI (energy return on investment) than energy-rich sources like oil (BBE). Luckily, wood is not the only bioresource that we have injected into our energy systems. Another source of bioenergy that we use comes from biomass waste. Deriving energy from biomass waste is more practical than doing so from wood because people produce 262 million tons of municipal solid waste per year (BBE).
    In the US, the average person wasted a pound of food every day. The healthier/stricter their diet, the more food waste they produced (cbsnews). Food, along with other types of biomass waste like paper, wood dust, can be used in the production of bioenergy through many different ways. Two common ways are through burning and the production of biogas. The burning of trash and biomass waste is typically done in energy plants. The burning waste is used to heat water into steam. This steam in turn can turn turbines that generate electricity through movement in a magnetic field (BBE). The advantages of this is that the waste is reduced to a more elementary state that can be better mixed into dirt. It is truly a process that eliminates two birds with one stone. The drawback to this process is, however, that the burning of a vastly diverse batch of waste can cause the emission of various harmful gases into the atmosphere. This can have an impact on nearby neighborhoods if not properly controlled. In addition, the burned product is not always safe to put into the ground.​​
         










Figure 1. (Technicaltoday)

The other way to use biomass waste is by producing biogas with it. This requires a anaerobic digester system that few cities have established. These systems typically will be using food waste along with treated wastewater, and other municipal waste to produce power or fuel. The digestion process involves fermentation of the feedstock to produce biogas. The mixture of biomass waste, often in the form of a ‘sludge-like’ mixture, is fermented in a chamber in the absence of oxygen. The product is a biogas that is about 60% methane (BBE). Similar to burning biomass waste, biogas can also be burned to produce electricity. Biogas production facilities can often be very efficient as they can send the biogas electricity produced to the powergrid while often still having enough to power the facility itself. Aside from electricity, the biogas produced can also be used as a gasoline replacement when made into compressed natural gas vehicle fuel. The main downside to this process are the odors produced!
