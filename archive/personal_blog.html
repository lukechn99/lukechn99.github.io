Bioenergy

Luke Chen, May 3, 2019


Bioenergy is the past, and it seems like we are moving towards making it the future as well. Wood was one of the first sources of bioenergy used. It was burned to provide light, warmth, and for cooking foods. In successive eras, oil, coal, and natural gas have largely taken the place of wood in providing energy for developed countries. However, taking sequestered carbon in coal, oil, and natural gas, and releasing it into the atmosphere as byproduct greenhouse gas emissions in the process of creating energy has caused severe climate change that will be hard to recover from. Bioenergy claims to address this problem by only releasing the recently sequestered carbon (like in tree wood) instead of putting carbon from below ground into the atmosphere. However, even using trees has a short term negative effect on the atmosphere because it simultaneously puts carbon into the atmosphere while also taking away the very source of carbon uptake that would balance the extra carbon: trees! In addition to this dilemma, there is also the fact that cutting down forests for energy is impractical because wood has a lower EROI (energy return on investment) than energy-rich sources like oil. Luckily, wood is not the only bioresource that we have injected into our energy systems. Another source of bioenergy that we use comes from biomass waste. Deriving energy from biomass waste is more practical than doing so from wood because people produce 262 million tons of municipal solid waste per year.

In the US, the average person wasted a pound of food every day. The healthier/stricter their diet, the more food waste they produced. Food, along with other types of biomass waste like paper, wood dust, can be used in the production of bioenergy through many different ways. Two common ways are through burning and the production of biogas. The burning of trash and biomass waste is typically done in energy plants. The burning waste is used to heat water into steam. This steam in turn can turn turbines that generate electricity through movement in a magnetic field. The advantages of this is that the waste is reduced to a more elementary state that can be better mixed into dirt. It is truly a process that eliminates two birds with one stone. The drawback to this process is, however, that the burning of a vastly diverse batch of waste can cause the emission of various harmful gases into the atmosphere. This can have an impact on nearby neighborhoods if not properly controlled. In addition, the burned product is not always safe to put into the ground.

The other way to use biomass waste is by producing biogas with it. This requires a anaerobic digester system that few cities have established. These systems typically will be using food waste along with treated wastewater, and other municipal waste to produce power or fuel. The digestion process involves fermentation of the feedstock to produce biogas. The mixture of biomass waste, often in the form of a ‘sludge-like’ mixture, is fermented in a chamber in the absence of oxygen. The product is a biogas that is about 60% methane. Similar to burning biomass waste, biogas can also be burned to produce electricity. Biogas production facilities can often be very efficient as they can send the biogas electricity produced to the powergrid while often still having enough to power the facility itself. Aside from electricity, the biogas produced can also be used as a gasoline replacement when made into compressed natural gas vehicle fuel. The main downside to this process are the odors produced!
